# Writing Patterns from Bybit Learn Corpus

> **Note:** This file should be updated with real data after running the corpus analyzer (analyze.py).
> The patterns below are initial guidelines based on the style guide and will be refined with actual data.

## Article length norms

- **Product Explainers**: 1,500–2,000 words. Longer articles (2,500+) are reserved for complex products with many steps.
- **Campaign Articles**: 800–1,200 words. Shorter and punchier.
- **General/Educational**: 1,200–1,800 words.

## Paragraph structure

- Average paragraph length: 2–4 sentences (40–80 words)
- Opening paragraphs tend to be shorter (1–2 sentences) to hook the reader
- Tutorial step paragraphs are longer (3–5 sentences) to fully explain each action
- Closing paragraphs: 2–3 sentences with a clear CTA

## Section ordering patterns

### Product Explainers (most common order)
1. Hook / problem statement (1 paragraph)
2. Key takeaways (bullet list)
3. "What is [Product]?" explanation
4. Step-by-step tutorial (the bulk of the article)
5. Tips or best practices
6. FAQ (optional, ~40% of articles include this)
7. Bottom line / conclusion with CTA

### Campaign Articles (most common order)
1. Energetic hook with prize/reward mention
2. Campaign overview
3. Details table (period, eligibility, prize pool)
4. How to participate (steps)
5. Prize breakdown
6. Terms and conditions
7. Closing CTA

## Intro paragraph formulas

Common opening patterns on Bybit Learn:
- **Problem-solution**: "Struggling with X? [Product] makes it easy to Y."
- **Opportunity**: "Looking for a way to X? Bybit's [Product] lets you Y."
- **Direct definition**: "[Product] is Bybit's [description]. Here's how to get started."
- **News hook** (campaigns): "Bybit is launching [Campaign] with a [prize] pool — here's everything you need to know."

## CTA patterns

- Product Explainers: "Ready to try [Product]? Head to [Bybit page] and get started today."
- Campaigns: "Don't miss out — join [Campaign] now and start earning your share of [prize pool]."
- Always end with a specific action, not a vague "learn more"

## Tone markers

- First person plural ("we") is rare — the voice is more instructional ("you")
- Questions are used sparingly in headings but effectively in intros
- Contractions are used naturally (don't, it's, you'll) — never overly formal
- Enthusiasm is expressed through word choice and sentence rhythm, not exclamation marks
- Exclamation marks: maximum one per article, typically in the CTA or opening hook
