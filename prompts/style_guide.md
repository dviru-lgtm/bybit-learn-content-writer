# Why do we have this guide?
The words we use convey our products, services and brand personality. Good copy ensures smooth user journeys, instills confidence and builds rapport with clients worldwide.

This guide is for team members drafting English copy in four areas:

- **Learn content**

- **Campaigns and events**
# UK or US spelling
We use US English and largely follow the AP Style Guide, with house deviations for clarity and UX readability.
### House deviations from AP
- No Oxford comma
- AM/PM in caps without periods (10AM, 4:20PM)
- No space before AM/PM
- Always display UTC
- Sentence case is default (except CTAs, product names, tokens)
- No emojis in UX or product copy
The reason is to maintain clarity and scannability based on prevailing UX writing best practices. The tenet is to make sure the reader can process the texts with the least effort possible.

# Copy principles
## Consistency 
Use the same word when describing one thing in the same copy. Be consistent with the tone, sentence structure and terms used. Example:

**Correct**
Header: To delete trades, contact us.
Footer: Learn more about how we delete trades.

**Incorrect**
Header: Join us in the latest campaign!
Footer: This event is valid for 2 weeks.

## “Less is more”
Good copywriting should convey the most meanings with the fewest words while being fully integrated into the user’s experience.

Hence, there is no need to tell the user everything. Tell them what is relevant to their action. Don’t use a long word if a shorter one will do, and don’t repeat what’s already known.

When in doubt, remember that clarity always comes first. Our aim is to funnel the reader to the intended action as efficiently as possible.

## Sentence structure
For descriptive copy, such as UX writing, use the following structure: [description 1] + [description 2]. [Benefits to the user]
**Correct: **This app can connect to other plugins and can track data in real-time. Use it anytime to manage your global business.
**Incorrect:** This app can connect to other plugins. You can use it anytime to manage your global business and it can allow you to track data in real-time.

For instructive copy, following the [To + objective] or [situation] + [action] structures. See the explanation below:

<image token="MtDMbdiFdowN99x4rn1uk6PUsI6" width="1200" height="620"/>


**Correct: **To use this function, activate it first in Settings.
**Incorrect:** Activate this function in Settings before trying to use it.
**Correct: **Error occurred. Please try again.
**Incorrect:** Please try again as an error has occurred.

### Sentence length
If you need to convey several messages, break them into multiple sentences. Never have a run-on sentence. According to the BBC, a sentence should never be longer than 16 words. 
**Correct: **For safety reasons, enter your verification code. If you’re still unable to upload your data, check your file format or contact us.
**Incorrect:** For safety reasons, enter your verification code, if you’re still unable to upload your data, please check your file format or consult our customer representative.

### Clarify “this” and “that”
Avoid starting a sentence with "this" or "that" unless it's immediately followed by the noun or if there’s a sentence in front. The key is to make sure the user knows what “this” is without much thinking.

## Context equals credibility
As UX writers, we often encounter situations where the wireframe and copy don’t necessarily answer the user’s questions or provide enough information.

Always think in the user’s shoes. If you see a spot that may benefit the user by providing extra information, or if you find a way to improve the way you lay out your copy, always let the designer know. 

Also, always let your users know why an action is necessary, and whenever necessary (say for an error message), include the next steps. 

Remember, we’re UX writers, not copy fixers. 

**Examples**
- For safety reasons, enter your verification code.
- Couldn’t upload data. Please check your file’s format.

Also, don’t spell out the actual UI component. Just describe the action and the result. 
**Correct: **Click **Continue** to finish
**Incorrect:** Click the **Continue** button at the bottom to finish

## Use simple words
Whenever you can use a simpler word, always do so. This means “make sure” instead of “ensure,” “check” instead of “verify*,” “get” instead of “obtain” etc.
**Note**: In the context of Identity Verification or anything formal, we use "verify" instead. But for anything else (e.g. mobile numbers or form field input) we use "check."
## Don’t use jargon
Technical terms don't always resonate. Descriptions should be simple, and in some cases literal, to avoid confusion when translating.

In this case, if you encounter a trading related technical concept, explain it in plain English unless it involves specific trading terminology. 

Remember, our tenet is to break down tedious crypto trading moments into something even our grandmothers can understand while helping users solve their problems.

## You vs We/Us vs Bybit
### Client servicing/Platform
We address our users and readers as “you,” and we write to them as if we’re having a direct conversation with them. But when referring to our brand products or programs, spell out “Bybit.”
**Examples**
- Thank you for joining the Bybit Affiliate Program. We look forward to witnessing your success.
- To continue, configure your account.

Avoid using technical jargon like “backend.” Keep your language conversational and natural.
**Correct: **Log in and access your report. 
**Incorrect:** Log in to the Bybit system backend and access your report.  

Hence, we say “Contact us” or “The Bybit Team” for our generic emails and communications.
If the email is coming from or needs to refer to a particular department, we need to specify which department. Use the “Contact our [department/entity]” format. For signoffs, use “The Bybit [department/team] Team” in title case.

## Please and sorry
### Platform/Learn content/Campaigns/Help center
Avoid using "please" and "sorry" liberally. Doing so makes us look old-school, grovelling and unreliable.

Reserve "please" for when the situation requires the user's extra attention. Use "sorry" only when we make a genuine mistake.  
**Correct: **
- To process your query, please tell us more about the last transaction you made with your account. 
- It seems we’ve made a mistake. Sorry for the inconvenience. 
**Incorrect:** 
- Please enter your username and password.  
- We’re sorry, we can’t open an account for you for compliance reasons.
- Sorry, this service is unavailable at this time.

### Client servicing
Besides helping customers and clients solve problems, client servicing team members also strive to provide a great experience while keeping the customers assured. Hence, unlike other scenarios, a small amount of “please” and “sorry” can help defuse any dissatisfaction that may otherwise occur:

**Correct: **To process your enquiry, could you please tell us more about your last transaction?
**Incorrect:** To process your enquiry, tell us more about your last transaction.

**Correct: **We’re sorry. For compliance reasons, we’re unable to open your account.
**Incorrect:** For compliance reasons, we’re unable to open your account.

## Sentence case
Sentence case improves readability and supports natural scanning by aligning with how people typically read and write. 

Research by Nielsen Norman Group and Microsoft shows users recognize words by their shapes, and sentence case preserves familiar contours, reducing cognitive load compared to title case or all caps. 

Leading organizations like Google also recommend sentence case in their UX guidelines because it’s easier to read, less distracting and creates a more user-friendly experience.

Hence, staying in line with UX writing best practices, we’re **now using sentence case for all copy** **except**:
- Specific terms (such as token names, campaign names, feature names and product names)
- Document names
- Campaign taglines
- CTAs
- UI strings (see below)

All other text, including titles, headings, labels, menu items, navigation components, app bars and buttons should use sentence case.
### UI strings
All product-wide headings use sentence case. CTAs remain Title Case. UI headers and labels follow sentence case unless stakeholders specify otherwise. Body texts, hover texts, toggles or any headers or labels not related to any existing journeys can still be in sentence case.

## Inclusive language
Avoid gendered pronouns whenever possible and use *they*, *their* or *them* instead of *he*, *she*, *him* or *her*. 
**Correct: **Ask your admin whether they can help.
**Incorrect: **Ask your admin whether he or she can help.  

When referring to someone with unknown gender or a non-binary person, the correct singular verb form for “they” is “they are” or “they think,” never “they is” or “they thinks.” 

If we’re describing an object owned by a person, avoid using wording that is definitely singular (like use *future* rather than *life/lives* when you say “educate a person and uplift their future”; *lives* may violate subject-verb agreement).

## That vs which
"That" is used to introduce restrictive or essential clauses. These clauses provide the necessary information to identify or define the noun being referred to. 
**Example:** The book that I bought yesterday is very interesting.

"Which" is used to introduce non-restrictive or non-essential clauses. These clauses provide additional information about the noun being referred to, but the sentence could still make sense without them. 
**Example:** The book, which I bought yesterday, is very interesting.

## Less vs fewer 
Use "fewer" for countable items, and "less" for uncountable quantities. Use "number" for countable items, and "amount" for uncountable quantities. 
**Examples:** Less than 10%, fewer than 15 people

## Copy vs copies
"Copy," when referring to written content, is a singular uncountable noun, so its plural form is also "copy." 
**Correct: **We need to finalize the **copy** for the homepage and the email campaign.
**Incorrect: **We need to finalize the **copies** for the homepage and the email campaign.

If you’re referring to multiple pieces of written content, it's still correct to use "copy," not "copies." However, if you're talking about physical duplicates of a document, book, or publication, you would use "copies".
## Latin abbreviations
Don’t put an "and" in front of the final item that precedes "etc." It always comes with a period. Don't use "and more."
**Example: **There are different ways to earn this token, such as airdrop, lucky draws etc.

For e.g. and i.e., remember to add periods before the letters. However, unlike the AP Style guide, we no longer need to add a comma after either case for clarity’s sake.
**Example: **There are different ways to earn this token, e.g. airdrop, lucky draws etc.

For vs., keep the period. 
## Countries and regions
To avoid geopolitical and reputational risks, never refer to Hong Kong, Taiwan and Macau as countries. 

When referring to mainland China, use “the Chinese Mainland," not "mainland China,” “China” or “PRC.” When referring to Hong Kong and Macau, use “Hong Kong” or “Hong Kong SAR” and “Macau” or “Macau SAR.” Don’t use “Hong Kong, China” or “Macau, China.” 

Refer to Taiwan simply as “Taiwan.”

Use “countries and regions” (or “countries or regions”) instead of “countries” when referring to geographical locations.

It’s always “the UK,” “the US,” “the Netherlands” and “the Philippines.” 

In terms of the Czech Republic, use “the Czech Republic” for an international audience. For a regional or local audience, use “Czechia.”For "Turkey," please use "Türkiye."

# Time and date
At Bybit, we follow the AP Style Guide for time, date and year.
## Date
Use the following sequence: **Month abbreviation, Date, Year, Time (12-hr clock) UTC**. For a date range, divide the dates with an en dash with space before and after. 
**Examples: **
- Jul 29, 2022, 10AM UTC
- Aug 8 – Aug 12, 2022
- Jul 29, 2022, 10AM UTC – Dec 31, 2023, 9AM UTC

However, there’s no need to mark “(midnight)” or “(noon)” anymore. You also don’t need to display days of the week (e.g. Wednesday).
## Time
We use the 12-hour format. AM and PM need to be all caps. There is no space between time numerals and AM/PM.
Use :00 only when necessary (e.g., 10AM). Omit it otherwise (e.g., 4:20PM, 9:05AM). Always display UTC unless explicitly writing for a user-local time context.
**Example: **12AM UTC, 4:20PM UTC

## Year
Always need to display the year when writing dates. Note that we never spell out years, even if they're at the beginning of a sentence.

To refer to a decade, add an "s" to the last preceding year that's divisible by 10, but never add an inverted comma.
**Example: **With the passing of Rep. John Lewis in 2020, we have lost the last member of the "Big Six" of the civil rights movement of the 1960s.

## Abbreviations
When we abbreviate time units in tables or UI strings, we follow the following principle:

**Less than 24 hours: **small letters, no space
**More than 24 hours: **capital letters, no space

## Active vs passive voice
Use active voice whenever possible. Passive voice can make the language verbose, distant and negative, which is the vibe we want to avoid. 

There are three instances in which passive voice may be used: 1) if it helps make a sentence flow better, 2) if it helps make a point come across more clearly, or 3) when the message is serious and needs a sterner and more formal tone. 
**Examples:**
- 1 customer selected
- Once configured, you can view referral data here

Meanwhile, 
“We take the protection of your store data very seriously, and guarantee it will not be revealed or shared with a third party.”

Can be rewritten as:
“We take your store's privacy very seriously. We’ll never reveal or share your data with a third party.”

A good rule of thumb is keeping active and passive voices at a 9:1 ratio. 

**Examples:**

## Brackets 
Brackets help add supplementary information to a sentence or a clause. Use brackets sparingly and avoid using them whenever you can.

- Blogs, navigation, pages
**Incorrect: **

## Hyphens and dashes 
### Hyphens
Hyphens, en dashes, and em dashes are different. 

Use a hyphen if you want to combine two concepts into one. In this case, the first word is usually a noun, and the second word is an adjective or an adverb. 
**Example:** self-doubting, dog-friendly, self-restraining person

Note that some common and specific terms are one word rather than two. 
**Example:** multicurrency, username, coworker 

For a hyphenated number that’s part of a clause, the number remains singular.
**Example:** 10-minute timeframe

### En dash
An en dash is longer than a hyphen and usually conveys a range or a pause. 
Use en dash for multi-word elements otherwise hard to hyphenate, emphasize a thought, or for indicating ranges of time. There should be no space before or after the en dash (except when it's for conveying a date range).

**Example: **High–net worth user, New York–based company
**Example: **The Bukom–Pulau Tekong Expressway
**Example: **2015 – 2017
**Example: **Oct 5, 2025 – Oct 7, 2025

### Em dash
Use em dash "—" for headers to mark off additional information that is not essential to understand the sentence (option+shift+hyphen). Keep the spacing before and after.
Yet, avoid using em dashes as they make our copy look "AI." Instead, use colons whenever you can. 
**Example:** Bybit is proud to present the biggest trading competition — the World Series of Trading 2022.

You can use an em dash as a substitute for commas, parentheses or colons when conveying additional information. 
**Example:** The team — which had been training for months — finally won the championship.

## Italics
Use italics for emphasis lighter than bolding. 
**Example:** Use *they*, *their* or *them* instead of *he*, *she*, *him* or *her*.

## Lists and bullet points
Begin a list with a verb and capitalize the first word. Don’t end with a period or any punctuation mark except for T&Cs. Also, we don't need to add an ", or" after the penultimate item of the list.
### Examples
With your Easyrank SEO, you can:
- Identify the right keywords
- See how your page rank
- Make your page more visible

Here are some of the functions of our new payment function:
- Pay and receive money globally
- Manage up to 30 currencies on one platform

## Meme coins
It’s written as two words. So, we say “meme coin,” not “memecoin.”

Use periods for full sentences in body text, emails and tooltips for clarity and consistency. However, avoid using periods when the copy is part of:
- A label
- A hover text
- A header
- A speech box/toast box
- Supporting text below a text field
- A banner ad copy (heading and subheading)
- A bullet point (except for T&Cs)

Use “double quotation marks.” Reserve ‘single quotation marks’ for quotes within a quote.
Always use English punctuation marks and leave space afterwards. **Never** use Chinese punctuation marks (such as 「」, 【】, 。, ，, ！,（）,《》) or put two punctuation marks together.

Also, avoid using exclamation marks. Only use them discreetly for campaign copy.

**Incorrect:** When setting up your transaction types., remember to insert your SWIFT code。Otherwise，there could be delays.

As a crypto platform, it’s okay to short form for some longer words for shorter copy, such as UI strings or banners. For example:

**Shortened: **Hi-res, sync, max/min (labels only), info (labels only)

For these cases, there is no need to add any periods (e.g. “max” instead of “max.”).

However, if you’re writing long-form copy or for a more serious setting, always use the full form.

## Popup error message
The header should show what’s happening – starting with a verb – with the fewest words possible, no more than 3. 

The body would give an extra explanation or context of the scenario. The whole point is to let the user grasp the issue and the possible solutions. Don’t repeat the title’s content, and only communicate what’s necessary. 

For simple issues
- Convey the situation and the solution
- Give enough context for the user to solve the problem themselves

For serious issues
- Refrain from using excessively strong wording
- Provide clear steps to solve the problem. If there’s no immediate solution, convey the issue’s impact and progress for a solution
- Apologize if we’re at fault

### Hint placeholders
When requesting users’ input, be clear about the format and what’s expected of them in the placeholder. Never repeat the title’s message. If there’s no need for a placeholder, don’t include a placeholder.

## Dialogs/Modals
When introducing irreversible or impactful actions, we would verify with the user beforehand. In this case, the dialog box/modal must illustrate the action and the consequence.

### Title
The title should contain a specific question, concisely explain what’s involved in the request, and provide clear actions – all in a handful of words.
**Correct: **Delete message?
**Incorrect: **Are you sure to delete this message?/Are you sure you want to delete this message?
**Incorrect: **Warning!

### Text
The body should give an extra explanation or context of the scenario. The whole point is to let the user grasp the issue and the possible solutions. Never repeat the title’s content and only communicate what’s necessary. 
**Correct: **You’ll need to start again if you wish to install this plugin later. 
**Incorrect: **This can’t be undone
**Incorrect: **Are you sure you want to delete this message?

### CTA
CTAs should propose an action, such as *Delete* or *Remove*. Affirmative actions should be on the right side of the screen, while dismissive actions (such as *Go back*) should be on the left.

Don’t use “yes” or “no.” If there are no actions needed, simply use *OK*.

Refer to this guide when drafting CTAs, which will be regularly updated.
**Correct:**

CTA2: Go Back

**Incorrect:**

CTA2: No

## Fail vs couldn't
We avoid “fail” in all user-facing copy (emails, UI strings, announcements, etc.) as it sounds harsh and assigns blame. Instead, use “couldn’t” or rephrase naturally.
**Correct:**
- Couldn't log in. Try again later.
- Authentication unsuccessful*
- To prevent a decision against your favor, please cooperate with the investigation and respond within the given timeframe.

*Note: "Unsuccessful" should be reserved for very short system messages where active phrasing would feel awkward (e.g. UI strings, email titles etc.)

**Incorrect:** 
- Login failed. Try again later.
- Authentication failed
- Please cooperate with the investigation and respond within the given timeframe. Failure to do so may result in a decision not in your favor. 

**Exceptions:**
- Use “failure to” only in formal legal or compliance copy where required.
- Use “fail/failed” in developer documentation, backend logs, or technical specs where it’s the standard convention.

## Hover text/Tooltip
<image token="TdZ2b28uko0eO4xqTN8uB2Z4sBg" width="714" height="313"/>


A tooltip (or hover text) provides additional context for a UI element. There are 2 kinds of tooltips: plain tooltip (1) and rich tooltip (2).
### Plain tooltips
Plain tooltips briefly describe a UI element. They're best used for labelling UI elements with no text, like icon-only buttons and fields.

This type is used for  quick definition explanation (e.g. what a term means). To better explain definitions and depending on the context, use the following sentence structure to begin:
- This refers to [xxx]
- [Noun] is a [xxx]

Ideally, a plain tooltip shouldn’t exceed 1 sentence. You also need a period at the end.

### Rich tooltips
Rich tooltips provide additional context about a UI element. They’re best used for longer text like definitions or explanations.

Unlike a plain tooltip, it’s used to explain more complex concepts or step-by-step updates. In this case, the copy can be longer and more comprehensive, but the tenet of conciseness and scannability remains. The point is to explain the concept in the sense of how it’s relevant to the user, ideally in 3 sentences or less.

If the copy text illustrates several ideas, use bullet points. Depending on the situation, adding a header is optional but is helpful. Remember to include a period at the end of the text.

There could be CTAs for this tooltip, such as:
- Next
- Previous
- OK
